# Primer to *The Islands of Sina Una* (5e Campaign Setting)

The Islands of Sina Una is a 5e campaign setting inspired by precolonial Philippine mythology and cultures, created by an all-Filipino team to introduce a mythical pre-colonial Philippine world to tabletop RPGs.[web:12][web:14][web:17][web:18] The name “Sina Una” means “those who came first,” referencing the precolonial eras rather than being an in‑world term.[web:13]

---

## 1. Big Picture: What Sina Una Is About

- A full 5e campaign setting (250+ pages) centered on Philippine myth, spirits, and precolonial lifeways.[web:12][web:15][web:18]  
- Intended both as:
  - A complete setting to run long campaigns in the islands.  
  - A toolbox of ancestries, classes, monsters, and lore you can port into other 5e games.[web:11][web:19]

### Core Themes

- **Animism and spirits**: Every place has spirits; communities negotiate, appease, and sometimes fight them.[web:11][web:14][web:18]  
- **Mortals between gods and spirits**: People survive by mediating between powerful beings, not by straightforward “good vs evil” crusades.[web:11][web:13]  
- **Cycles, ancestors, and the river of the dead**: Death is a journey down the Black River; some spirits return as ancestors or even as dragonborn-like beings.[web:11][web:13]  
- **Impending cosmic danger**: Celestial Eaters like Bakunawa, the moon‑eating serpent, threaten the world and its moon.[web:11][web:18][web:19]  
- **Local power and community**: Authority is local (datu, timawa) rather than empires or kingdoms; communities matter more than distant states.[web:2][web:13]

## 2. Cosmology and Mythic Structure

The setting’s cosmology draws heavily from Philippine mythic concepts, adapted to 5e.

### 2.1 The World as a Shell

- The world is shaped like a clamshell or “shell”:  
  - Upper half: realm of gods and sky.  
  - Lower half: ocean and the mortal world’s seas.  
  - The horizon is the rim where the two meet.[web:11]  
- The Black River flows across the world and terminates at the horizon, where spirits cross into the afterlife.[web:11]

### 2.2 Creation and Early History

- Originally, reality was scattered fragments of land within a churning sea.[web:13]  
- The god Bathala shaped the world into something coherent, forming peoples from natural features and spirit-material:
  - Elves: once plant and flower spirits taking mortal form.  
  - Gnomes: rising from the earth itself.[web:13]  
- After initial creation, the gods largely withdrew, leaving mortals in a broken world to negotiate with spirits to rebuild.[web:13]

### 2.3 Gods and Divine Families

The cosmology emphasizes divine kinship networks and domains tied to natural features and human experiences.

- The sky god has two notable families:  
  - First wife: the Goddess of Lost Things.  
    - Later marries the God of Mountains.  
    - Their children include gods of:
      - Lovers and childbirth  
      - Death and mortality  
      - Harvest and fertility  
      - Transition between life and death[web:11]  
  - Second wife: a mortal woman.  
    - Their children are the sun and the moon.[web:11]

- Other gods govern:
  - Soil  
  - Forests  
  - Waves  
  - Fire and volcanoes  
  - Storms, lightning, thunder  
  - Hunters[web:11]

### 2.4 Ancestors, Spirits, and the Afterlife

- Mortal spirits travel down the Black River to the afterlife, which is the “flip side” of the mortal world on the shell.[web:11]  
- During specific festivals, some ancestors are allowed to return, often inhabiting reptiles (like lizards or crocodiles) to observe and interact with the living.[web:11]  
- Spirits that refuse to travel downriver can become dangerous; some who swim upriver may be reincarnated as dragonborn-like beings (the umalagad), with crocodilian or serpentine traits.[web:11][web:13]

### 2.5 The Celestial Eaters

The Celestial Eaters are cosmological threats tied to eclipses and apocalyptic omens.

List of key Celestial Eaters:[web:11]

- **Arimaonga** – A cosmic lion who playfully hunts and tries to eat the moon, without particular malice.[web:11]  
- **Bakunawa** – A vast serpent jealous of the beauty of the sky gods and hungry to devour the moon.[web:11][web:18]  
- **Minokawa** – An eagle who hunts the moon as part of its nature, similar to Arimaonga.[web:11]  
- **Tambanokano** – A crab, abandoned child of the moon, seeking revenge.[web:11]  
- **Tambanokua** – A spider fascinated by what will happen if the moon is gone.[web:11]

They embody recurring cosmic crises rather than one‑off boss monsters; some do not even have stat blocks, emphasizing their narrative weight over tactical combat.[web:11][web:19]

## 3. The Islands and Their Societies

### 3.1 Geography of the Setting

- The known world centers on seven major islands forming an archipelago in a vast ocean.[web:2][web:11][web:18]  
- Each island is detailed with:
  - Demographics (which ancestries live there)  
  - Geography and climate  
  - Lifestyle and culture  
  - Major settlements  
  - Local spirits and supernatural features[web:11][web:18]  
- Weather and environment vary dramatically among islands, shaping very different local cultures, economies, and threats.[web:11]

### 3.2 Society, Governance, and Everyday Life

- The islands share a common language and some broad customs, but governance is extremely local.[web:2][web:13]  
- Typical political structures:
  - **Datu** – Local leader acting as lawmaker, judge, and administrator of protection and resources.[web:2][web:13]  
  - **Timawa** – Warriors who protect the community and enforce the datu’s authority in exchange for patronage and support.[web:2][web:13]  
- Community life revolves around:
  - Agriculture and fishing  
  - Sea trade between islands  
  - Ritual relationships with local spirits  
  - Festivals that mark cosmic and agricultural cycles[web:11][web:18]

### 3.3 Seafaring and Trade

- Seafaring is crucial for trade, diplomacy, and exploration between islands.[web:2]  
- The intensity and style of seafaring vary:
  - Some groups specialize in long voyages and open‑ocean navigation.  
  - Others focus on local coastal travel, reflecting different lifestyles and economic roles.[web:2]

## 4. Player Options: Characters in Sina Una

*(Note: This section is a structural overview, not a reproduction of mechanical text.)*

The book devotes a full chapter to character creation in the setting, including ancestries, classes, subclasses, backgrounds, feats, equipment, magic items, and spells.[web:11][web:15]

### 4.1 Ancestries and Peoples

- Mortals in Sina Una include familiar 5e ancestries, often re‑flavored or given mythic origins:
  - Elves as former plant and flower spirits.  
  - Gnomes rising from the earth.  
  - Dragonborn‑like umalagad as reincarnated ancestor-spirits.[web:11][web:13]  
- Each island’s demographics section indicates which ancestries are most prevalent and how they fit local culture.[web:11]

### 4.2 Classes, Subclasses, and New Base Classes

- Product descriptions and reviews highlight:
  - 2 new base classes.  
  - 12 new subclasses for existing 5e classes.[web:15][web:11]  
- Character options lean into:
  - Spirit mediators (babaylan-style shamans and ritualists).  
  - Warriors tied to local spirits, gods, or ancestral oaths.  
  - Seafarers and monster hunters shaped by the archipelagic environment.[web:11][web:13][web:19]

### 4.3 Backgrounds, Feats, Equipment, and Magic

- Backgrounds position PCs as:
  - Members of local communities (fishers, farmers, boat builders, traders).  
  - Spirit intermediaries or apprentices to shamans.  
  - Veterans of conflicts with aswangs or other monsters.[web:11][web:19]  
- Equipment and magic items reflect:
  - Traditional weapons and tools adjusted to 5e rules.  
  - Ritual objects used in animist practice and spirit pacts.  
- Spells often focus on:
  - Communicating with and bargaining with spirits.  
  - Weather, sea, and storm manipulation.  
  - Curses, blessings, and protective rituals.[web:11][web:19]

## 5. Monsters, Spirits, and Threats

### 5.1 Monster Chapter Overview

One chapter is devoted to creatures of the islands.

- 37 monster stat blocks for various creature types.  
- 16 stat blocks for spirits.  
- 18 stat blocks for mundane animals.[web:19]  
- Celestial Eaters are mostly treated as apocalyptic entities without standard stat blocks, reinforcing their role as story-level threats.[web:11][web:19]

### 5.2 Types of Creatures

- **Aswang and related monsters**:
  - Rooted in Philippine folklore, these include shape‑shifting, flesh‑eating, or soul‑stealing beings that prey on mortals.[web:11][web:19]  
- **Giants and primordial beings**:
  - Named giants helped shape the islands’ geography, sometimes unintentionally, and were tasked with protecting mortals from aswangs.[web:11]  
  - Angngalo, patriarch of these giants, failed in his duties, leaving them solitary and sullen.[web:11]  
- **Spirits (various)**:
  - Spirits of land, sea, weather, and specific local features can be allies, patrons, or antagonists.[web:11][web:19]  

### 5.3 Example Creature: Tigbalan

- The **Tigbalan** (CR 6) is a trickster fey that guards wilderness spaces.[web:19]  
- Typical behavior:
  - Plays dangerous pranks using illusions.  
  - Leads intruders into hazards, or gets them lost until they die of exposure, fear, or starvation.[web:19]  
- Motivation:
  - Views itself as a guardian of its forest or territory.  
  - Sees encroaching outsiders as a threat to flora and natural balance.[web:19]  
- Key interaction detail:
  - Travelers who politely ask permission to pass are usually allowed through safely.[web:19]

## 6. Running Games in Sina Una

### 6.1 Campaign Tone and Style

- Campaigns are often about:
  - Protecting communities from supernatural threats.  
  - Negotiating with spirits and balancing obligations between gods, ancestors, and living people.  
  - Responding to omens and movements of the Celestial Eaters (eclipses, strange tides, cosmic signs).[web:11][web:13][web:18][web:19]  
- Instead of standard dungeon-crawl tropes, the focus leans toward:
  - Social obligations and communal responsibility.  
  - Rituals, festivals, and seasonal cycles.  
  - Travel between islands and dealing with different local customs.[web:11][web:18]

### 6.2 Using Sina Una as a Toolbox

Even if you do not run the default islands, the book is a rich source of:

- Myth‑based monsters drawn from Philippine folklore.  
- Spirit‑centric class options and magic.  
- Setting structures (shell world, Black River, Celestial Eaters) that you can reskin into other cosmologies.[web:11][web:13][web:19]  
- Cultural frameworks for:
  - Local governance (datu, timawa, community councils).  
  - Animist relationships with land and ancestors.[web:2][web:11][web:13]

### 6.3 Themes to Respect at the Table

Because Sina Una is built from real cultural traditions and myths:

- Treat myths and rituals with respect; they are not generic fantasy “flavor.”[web:14][web:17]  
- Center local communities and their perspectives rather than outsider colonial narratives.  
- Use it as an opportunity to:
  - Highlight Filipino worldviews and storytelling traditions.  
  - Engage with precolonial contexts instead of default Western medieval frames.[web:13][web:14]

## 7. Structural Overview of the Book

Reviews and previews describe the book as organized roughly into four major chapters plus appendices.[web:11][web:15]

- **Chapter 1 – People and Belief**  
  - History, adventuring, animism, afterlife, gods, and overall themes.[web:11][web:13]  

- **Chapter 2 – The World and Islands**  
  - Details of the seven islands, ocean, and regions beyond the horizon; demographics, geography, climate, lifestyle, settlements, and spirits for each.[web:11][web:18]  

- **Chapter 3 – Characters and Magic**  
  - Ancestries, classes, subclasses, backgrounds, feats, equipment, magic items, and spells.[web:11][web:15]  

- **Chapter 4 – Monsters and Spirits**  
  - Bestiary with monsters, spirits, animals, and notes on Celestial Eaters and legendary beings like giants.[web:11][web:19]  

- **Appendices**  
  - Likely include indexes, reference tables, and possibly additional lore or tools to integrate the setting with broader 5e material.[web:11][web:15]

## 8. Quick Primer Checklist for a New GM

When prepping to run Sina Una, you can focus on:

1. **Cosmology basics**  
   - Shell world, Black River, divine families, ancestors, and Celestial Eaters.[web:11][web:13]  

2. **Local community focus**  
   - Understand how a datu and timawa operate, what your starting community values, and how spirits fit into everyday life.[web:2][web:11][web:13]  

3. **One or two islands in depth**  
   - Pick the starting island, learn its climate, demographics, local spirits, and key tensions.[web:11][web:18]  

4. **Signature monsters and spirits**  
   - Choose a handful of creatures (aswangs, tigbalan, a local spirit) that express the setting’s themes.[web:11][web:19]  

5. **Early plot hooks**  
   - A disrupted festival where ancestors cannot cross.  
   - Rumors of a Celestial Eater omen (eclipse, tidal anomaly).  
   - A datu seeking intermediaries to negotiate with a furious forest spirit.  
   - A timawa warband missing after hunting aswangs.[web:11][web:13][web:18][web:19]  

This framework lets you start a campaign or a short arc that feels distinctly Sina Una while staying manageable during prep.

- Read the introduction and setting chapters to internalize the cosmology, social structures, and tone.[web:11][web:13][web:18]  
- Decide if you’ll:
  - Run a native Sina Una campaign, or
