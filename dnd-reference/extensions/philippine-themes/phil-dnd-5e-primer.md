# Philippine-Themed D&D 5e Campaign Primer

A Philippine‑themed D&D 5e campaign works best when you ground your world in Filipino culture, history, and mythology, then plug that into 5e’s rules and pacing. [web:9][web:16]

---

## 1. Choose Your Philippine Flavor

Decide what “version” of the Philippines your setting reflects:

- Timeframe:
  - Pre‑colonial barangay‑era (animistic, independent communities, strong ties to nature spirits). [web:9][web:14]
  - Spanish‑period fantasy (friars, revolts, trade, syncretic religion). [web:18]
  - Modern / urban fantasy (similar to *Trese* or Sina Una’s later‑era inspirations). [web:9][web:16]

- Scope:
  - A single island.
  - One region (e.g., “fantasy Luzon + Visayas”).
  - A fully homebrewed archipelago, similar to *The Islands of Sina Una*. [web:14][web:16][web:18]

Use Philippines‑like geography (volcanoes, rice terraces, mangroves, coral islands, monsoon coasts) and rename them with Filipino‑inspired terms to feel familiar but still fantastical. [web:14][web:18]

---

## 2. Use Filipino Mythology and Folklore as Core Pillars

Anchor your gods, monsters, and conflicts in Filipino myth:

- Mythic beings and creatures:
  - Creator or high god similar to Bathala.
  - Bakunawa, the serpent that threatens the moon. [web:10]
  - Diwata (nature and place spirits).
  - Aswang variants, Manananggal, Tikbalang, Nuno sa Punso, and other regional monsters. [web:9][web:10][web:13]

- Themes:
  - Animism: everything has a spirit or “may bantay.” [web:9]
  - Balance between people, ancestors, and nature.
  - Community‑centered ethics: hiya, utang‑na‑loob, pakikisama, bayanihan woven into social expectations and story consequences. [web:9][web:14]

Example hooks:
- The party must appease a Diwata angered by logging a sacred forest.
- A Bakunawa‑linked cult plans a ritual to “eat the moon,” causing tides and storms to worsen. [web:9][web:16]

---

## 3. Borrow from Existing Filipino 5e Resources

You can save prep time by pulling from existing Philippine‑inspired 5e material:

- The Islands of Sina Una (Hit Point Press):
  - 5e‑compatible campaign setting heavily inspired by pre‑colonial Filipino myths. [web:14][web:16][web:18]
  - Includes new ancestries, subclasses, spells, monsters, and a pantheon.
  - Provides lore for animist practices, spirit pacts, and regional cultures.

- Islands & Aswangs (homebrew/online content):
  - Filipino folklore‑inspired supplement with new ancestries, class options, and aswang‑type monsters. [web:11][web:13]

How to use them:
- Reskin or directly import monsters, races, and subclasses.
- Lift story seeds, regional ideas, or pantheons as templates.
- Use their structure (regions, spirits, cultural notes) as a scaffold for your own archipelago. [web:14][web:15]

## 4. Build a Focused Mini‑Setting (1–3 Regions)

Keep the sandbox small but rich at first:

1. Pick 3–5 key locations:
   - Coastal barangay town (trade, fishing, politics).
   - Highland or rice‑terrace village (ancestral spirits, rituals).
   - Volcano‑temple or crater lake shrine (powerful Diwata or dragon‑like beings).
   - Enchanted forest (home to Nuno, Tikbalang, and diwata courts).
   - Sunken reef or coral city (sea spirits, merfolk‑equivalents). [web:14][web:16]

2. Attach a core problem to each:
   - Tree‑cutting or mining has angered a Diwata.
   - A quasi‑colonial power imposes harsh taxes and forced labor, sparking a hidden resistance. [web:18]
   - An Aswang cult kidnaps children or steals bodies before burial. [web:10][web:13]
   - Fisherfolk report that a Bakunawa‑like creature blocks sea routes. [web:10]

3. Make at least one conflict about community survival or dignity:
   - Villagers risk famine if taboo fishing grounds are closed.
   - A barangay risks losing ancestral land to a foreign‑backed landlord.
   - A family feud threatens to split the town during storm season. [web:9][web:14]

These stakes fit Philippine values and give players moral weight beyond gold and loot. [web:9][web:14]

## 5. Light 5e Mechanics Reskinning

You do not have to rewrite 5e; mostly re‑flavor it:

- Ancestries (Races):
  - Default to Human as the “baseline.”
  - Add a few spirit‑touched or culture‑specific options (e.g., forest‑blessed clan, sea‑folk, Anito‑touched), using mechanics from Sina Una‑style ancestries for inspiration. [web:14][web:18]
  - You can also import ancestries directly from *The Islands of Sina Una* and rename them to fit your world. [web:16]

- Classes and Subclasses:
  - Fighters flavored as arnis or eskrima warriors, shield‑and‑spear barangay defenders, or guerrilla fighters. [web:14]
  - Clerics and paladins as priests of a Bathala‑like creator or dedicated to specific Diwata; alternatively, spirit‑mediums bound to Anito. [web:9][web:16]
  - Warlocks as spirit‑pacted: patron is a volcano spirit, sea dragon, ancestral Diwata, or Bakunawa itself. [web:9][web:18]
  - Druids as babaylan‑style or katalonan‑style ritualists channeling nature and ancestor spirits. [web:9][web:14]

- Magic and Religion:
  - Spellcasting reflects folk practices: offerings, chants, herbs, and ritual dances.
  - Sacred sites: balete trees, springs, caves, shrines on hills, ancestral burial grounds. [web:9]
  - Priests track taboos; breaking them may cause disadvantage, curses, or angry spirits. [web:9][web:14]

- Monsters:
  - Reskin fey creatures as Diwata and nature spirits. [web:9][web:14]
  - Reskin vampires as Manananggal; give them flight and detachable torsos. [web:13]
  - Use Oni, hags, and fiends as templates for Aswang variants. [web:13]
  - Use centaur or satyr stat blocks for Tikbalang variants, adjusting flavor and abilities. [web:7][web:13]

## 6. Session 1: Simple Philippine Hooks

Give your first adventure a clear, culturally grounded hook:

- Harvest Festival Gone Wrong:
  - The barangay holds a harvest festival with dances, lechon, and offerings to a Diwata of the fields.
  - Omens appear: animals panic, a red moon rises, a Bakunawa shape crosses the sky, or a sacred tree bleeds sap. [web:9][web:10]
  - The elder asks the PCs to investigate a new logging camp, mine, or unblessed shrine that may have broken a taboo. [web:9][web:14]

- Escort the Offering:
  - PCs escort a ceremonial offering (rice, animals, crafted goods, ancestral heirlooms) to a mountaintop or crater lake shrine. [web:9]
  - The usual path is blocked by landslides, new monsters, or a rival community. [web:14]
  - Along the way they meet Nuno sa Punso, Tikbalang pranksters, or an Aswang scout testing their strength. [web:7][web:13]

- Missing Children of the Barangay:
  - Children vanish at night; rumors blame Aswangs or forest spirits slighted by the new mayor’s building projects. [web:13]
  - PCs must track clues through chanting circles, old midwives, and suspicious outsiders. [web:9]
  - The resolution can be tragic horror, social drama, or heroic rescue, depending on tone.

These kinds of hooks give you space to expand the world as the campaign grows, instead of front‑loading all worldbuilding. [web:17][web:19]

## 7. Tone, Values, and Table Expectations

Discuss with players:

- Tone:
  - Heroic epic (Malakas‑and‑Maganda‑scale stories).
  - Gritty folk horror (Aswangs, curses, lost barangays).
  - Pulpy adventure (sea voyages, volcano temples, pirates). [web:14][web:18]

- Themes:
  - Family, community reputation, and *utang‑na‑loob* matter as much as treasure. [web:9]
  - Encouraging bayanihan: PCs help communities and gain allies, protection, and blessings.
  - Consequences for disrespecting elders, spirits, and sacred spaces. [web:9][web:14]

Encourage players to tie their backstories to barangays, clans, or Diwata patrons so every quest feels personal and grounded in the Philippine‑inspired setting. [web:9][web:16]
