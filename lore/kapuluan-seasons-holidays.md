# Kapuluan — Seasons, Holidays, Ceremonies & Rituals

> *As told by Lakan-Diwa (senior Babaylan), Datu Malaya, Guro Awit (song-keeper), and Kapitan Habagat. Compiled by Pip Tanglethorn and Orindel Ashwick. Day 710, 1256 AS.*

---

## Seasons

Kapuluan has **two major seasons** and **two transitional periods**, driven by the monsoon cycle:

| Season | Local Name | Days (approx.) | Character |
|--------|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Dry Season** | **Tag-init** (Time of Heat) | Days 91–210 | Hot, calm seas, clear skies. Peak sailing, fishing, building, trading. The "open" season — everything happens. |
| **Wet Season** | **Tag-ulan** (Time of Rain) | Days 271–360 + 1–60 | Monsoon rains. Heavy storms. Seas rough. Villages hunker. Coral grows fastest in the warm rain. The "growing" season. |
| **Transition (Dry→Wet)** | **Pagbabago** (The Changing) | Days 211–270 | Winds shift. Humidity builds. Last harvests. Preparation for storms. The Bakunawa is traditionally most restless during this period. |
| **Transition (Wet→Dry)** | **Pagbubukang-liwayway** (The Dawning) | Days 61–90 | Rains ease. Seas calm. First clear skies. Planting season begins. Renewal. |

**Current season (Day 710):** Early Tag-init (Dry Season). Best weather. Best sailing. Best time for construction.

## The Calendar — Buwan (Moons)

Kapuluan counts time by **lunar months (Buwan)** — 12 moons per year, ~30 days each. Each moon has a name tied to the sea, the harvest, or the stars.

| # | Moon Name | Days | Season | Character |
|---|-----------|------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | **Buwan ng Ulan** (Moon of Rain) | 1–30 | Tag-ulan (Wet) | Heaviest rains. Stay indoors. Weaving, storytelling, repair work. |
| 2 | **Buwan ng Tubig** (Moon of Water) | 31–60 | Tag-ulan (Wet) | Flooding. Rivers high. Coral grows fastest. Babaylan healing season. |
| 3 | **Buwan ng Binhi** (Moon of Seeds) | 61–90 | Pagbubukang-liwayway | Rains ease. Rice planting begins. First calm seas. Renewal ceremonies. |
| 4 | **Buwan ng Tanim** (Moon of Planting) | 91–120 | Tag-init (Dry) | Full planting. Terraces flooded. Coral-singers repair winter storm damage. |
| 5 | **Buwan ng Layag** (Moon of Sails) | 121–150 | Tag-init (Dry) | Peak sailing season opens. Voyagers depart on long journeys. Trade begins. |
| 6 | **Buwan ng Araw** (Moon of Sun) | 151–180 | Tag-init (Dry) | Longest days. Hottest. Peak fishing. The sea is calmest. **Midsummer festival.** |
| 7 | **Buwan ng Ani** (Moon of Harvest) | 181–210 | Tag-init (Dry) | Rice harvest. Fruit harvest. Abundance. **Harvest festival.** |
| 8 | **Buwan ng Hangin** (Moon of Wind) | 211–240 | Pagbabago (Changing) | Winds shift. Storms begin. Last trading voyages. Boats hauled for repair. |
| 9 | **Buwan ng Alon** (Moon of Waves) | 241–270 | Pagbabago (Changing) | Seas roughen. The Bakunawa's traditional restless period. Coral-singers strengthen the reef. **Bakunawa vigil.** |
| 10 | **Buwan ng Dilim** (Moon of Darkness) | 271–300 | Tag-ulan (Wet) | Shortest days. Heaviest storms. The deep season. Deep Fishers go deepest now — the deep water is warmest. |
| 11 | **Buwan ng Tala** (Moon of Stars) | 301–330 | Tag-ulan (Wet) | Despite the rain, the stars are brightest between storms. Navigators recalibrate. **Star festival.** |
| 12 | **Buwan ng Pag-asa** (Moon of Hope) | 331–360 | Tag-ulan (Wet) | Last rains. The promise of dry season. **Year's End celebration.** Planning for the new year. |

## Major Holidays & Festivals

### 1. Pista ng Binhi — Festival of Seeds (Day ~75, Buwan ng Binhi)

**When:** Early in the Moon of Seeds, as the rains ease.
**What:** The planting festival. Every island celebrates the first rice planting of the year. The Babaylan bless the seed rice. The first seedlings are planted by the eldest farmer in each community. Children scatter flower petals on the flooded terraces.
**Ritual:** The Babaylan chants the **Dasal ng Lupa** (Prayer of the Earth) — asking the soil to receive the seed. The coral-singers hum a growth-tone into the reef — the coral's growth season begins simultaneously.
**Food:** Fresh rice cakes (puto), coconut milk sweets, the first fruits of the season.
**Duration:** 1 day per island, but staggered — the festival moves south through the archipelago over 2 weeks as each island plants.

### 2. Pista ng Layag — Festival of Sails (Day ~125, Buwan ng Layag)

**When:** The opening of sailing season.
**What:** The Voyagers' festival. Every seaworthy boat is launched, blessed, and raced. The fastest boat wins bragging rights for the year. Kapitan Habagat has won 14 times (he claims 15 — one is disputed).
**Ritual:** The Babaylan chants the **Basbas ng Dagat** (Blessing of the Sea) over each boat. The coral-singers grow a fresh coral garland for each prow. The first boat to leave the harbor carries a coral offering to the open ocean — dropped overboard at the reef's edge as thanks to the sea.
**Food:** Seafood feast — kinilaw, inihaw, sinigang. Tuba flows freely. Boat-hull drumming and dancing on the seawalls.
**Duration:** 3 days. Racing on Day 1. Feasting on Day 2. Departure of the long-voyage fleet on Day 3.
**Dawnwatch note:** The Tidecaller would be welcome to race. Revelle would want to know this.

### 3. Pista ng Araw — Midsummer Festival (Day ~165, Buwan ng Araw)

**When:** The longest day. Midsummer.
**What:** The biggest celebration of the year. Every island participates. Dagat-Puso hosts the central festival — thousands gather. Music, dancing, feasting, competitions (diving, swimming, canoe racing, weaving, cooking). The coral-singers perform their greatest works. The Babaylan conduct the **Ritwal ng Liwanag** (Ritual of Light) — at sunset, every coral in the reef is sung to maximum bioluminescence. The entire archipelago glows.
**Ritual:** At noon, the Datu council renews the **Pangako ng Kapuluan** (Promise of Kapuluan) — the collective oath that binds the islands: *"We are many. We are one. The ocean connects us. The coral remembers us."* Every Datu speaks it. Every person present repeats it.
**Food:** The grandest kamayan of the year. Every dish Kapuluan knows. Lechon (whole roasted pig) is the centerpiece. Bibingka towers. Tuba rivers.
**Duration:** 5 days. The longest festival. Islands send delegations to Dagat-Puso.

### 4. Pista ng Ani — Harvest Festival (Day ~195, Buwan ng Ani)

**When:** Rice harvest completion.
**What:** Thanksgiving. The terraces are golden. The rice is cut, threshed, stored. Every community celebrates the abundance. The Rice Builders lead — this is their festival.
**Ritual:** The **Pasasalamat sa Lupa** (Gratitude to the Earth) — the Babaylan and the eldest farmer walk the terraces together, touching each level, thanking the mountain for its soil and water. A portion of the first harvest is returned to the terrace — scattered on the highest level as an offering.
**Food:** New rice — the first meal from the fresh harvest. Cooked simply, with salt and fish, to honor the grain itself. Then: everything else. The harvest feast is second only to Midsummer.
**Duration:** 2 days.

### 5. Pagpupuyat ng Bakunawa — Bakunawa Vigil (Day ~255, Buwan ng Alon)

**When:** The Moon of Waves. Traditionally when the Bakunawa stirs.
**What:** A solemn observance, not a celebration. The coral-singers gather at the southern reef edge and sing the **Awit ng Pagtulog** (Lullaby of the Deep) — the traditional lullaby. The Deep Fishers keep watch on the trench. The Babaylan read the tides for signs.
**Ritual:** The **Bantay ng Kailaliman** (Watch of the Deep) — Deep Fishers dive to the trench edge at dawn and report what they feel. If the Bakunawa is still — peace. If it stirs — the full lullaby is performed.
**Post-Dawnwatch update:** Now that the Bakunawa is harmonized, the vigil may evolve. Lakan-Diwa suggests: "The vigil should continue — but as gratitude, not fear. We sing not to put it to sleep, but to remind it that it is not alone. The Awit ng Tulay replaces the Awit ng Pagtulog."
**Food:** Simple. Fish and rice. The vigil is not a feast. It is a watch.
**Duration:** 1 night (sunset to sunrise).

### 6. Pista ng Tala — Star Festival (Day ~315, Buwan ng Tala)

**When:** The Moon of Stars. Between monsoon storms, the sky clears briefly and the stars are at their brightest.
**What:** The Voyagers' sacred festival. Navigation is recalibrated. Star-songs are sung. Young navigators are tested — they must recite their star-songs from memory under the open sky. Those who pass are certified. Tala was certified at this festival at age 19 — the youngest in a generation.
**Ritual:** The **Pagsubok ng Tala** (Trial of the Stars) — the navigation test. Conducted on open water, at night, without instruments. The candidate must navigate between two islands using only stars, waves, and the feeling of the sea. Failure means another year of training. Success means the star-map tattoos begin.
**Food:** Midnight feast on the water — eaten in canoes under the stars. Kinilaw (raw fish — "star food," eaten cold under cold light).
**Duration:** 3 nights.

### 7. Pista ng Pag-asa — Year's End / Festival of Hope (Day ~350, Buwan ng Pag-asa)

**When:** End of the wet season. The last rains. The promise of renewal.
**What:** Year's End. Reflection, gratitude, hope. Every island lights bonfires on the beach — the fires visible across the water, island to island, a chain of light across the archipelago. The Babaylan sing the **Awit ng Bagong Simula** (Song of New Beginning).
**Ritual:** At midnight, every fire is extinguished simultaneously (coordinated by coral-network pulse). Total darkness. The entire archipelago goes dark. Then — the coral-singers sing the reef to maximum bioluminescence. The ocean itself lights up. Blue, green, pink — the reef glowing beneath the dark water, visible from every shore. The new year begins not with fire but with the ocean's own light.
**Food:** The last feast of the year. Everything preserved — smoked fish, dried fruit, fermented foods. Tuba. Bibingka. And the first dish of the new year: fresh kinilaw, prepared at midnight, eaten as the reef glows.
**Duration:** 1 night (sunset to dawn).
**Dawnwatch note:** This is the Kapuluan equivalent of Ember Night. The Dawnwatch should be here for this if possible.

## Lifecycle Ceremonies

### Birth — Pagsilang
- The Babaylan delivers the child. The first sound the baby hears is the Babaylan's **Awit ng Buhay** (Song of Life) — a personal melody composed for that child alone, never repeated for another. The melody is based on the tidal pattern at the moment of birth.
- The umbilical cord is buried beneath a coral growth — the child is literally rooted to the reef.
- The community brings food for the mother for 40 days (**pag-aalaga** — the caring period).

### Coming of Age — Pagdating ng Gulang
- At 14–16, young people undergo their **Pagsubok** (Trial) — specific to their people. Coral-singers must grow their first coral structure. Voyagers must complete a solo navigation. Deep Fishers must dive to 100 feet. Rice Builders must plant and harvest one terrace alone.
- Upon passing, the first tattoos are earned — the foundation patterns that will be built upon for life.

### Marriage — Pag-iisang Dibdib (Joining of Hearts)
- The Babaylan officiates. The couple stands in the ocean — waist-deep, at sunset. Their hands are bound with coral-thread (living, flexible). The Babaylan sings the **Awit ng Dalawa** (Song of Two) — a melody that weaves the couple's personal birth-songs into one.
- The community builds the couple's first home in a single day — **Bayanihan** at its most literal. Everyone carries a piece of the house.
- Feast: 3 days. The entire island.

### Death — Pagpanaw (Passing)
- The Babaylan sings the **Awit ng Pahinga** (Song of Rest) — guiding the spirit to the sea. The body is placed in a coral boat (grown specifically for this purpose) and set adrift at sunset. The coral-singers sing the boat to the reef's edge, where the current carries it to the open ocean.
- The deceased's personal birth-song is sung one final time — then never again. The melody returns to the sea.
- Mourning: 9 days. White clothing. No music except the Babaylan's chants.

## Daily Rituals

| Ritual | When | What |
|--------|------|------|
| **Pagbati sa Araw** (Greeting the Sun) | Dawn | Face east. Open hands. One breath. Acknowledge the day. Universal — every Kapuluan person does this. |
| **Panalangin bago Kumain** (Prayer before Eating) | Every meal | Touch the food. Thank the sea, the soil, the hands that prepared it. Brief — 5 seconds. But never skipped. |
| **Awit ng Gabi** (Evening Song) | Sunset | The coral-singers hum the reef to bioluminescence. Not a formal ritual — just habit. The reef glows brighter when they sing. So they sing every evening. |
| **Pahintulot** (Permission) | Before any taking (hunting, fishing, mining, harvesting) | Ask the land/sea/mountain. State what you will take. Promise to waste nothing. The Dawnwatch has adopted this fully. |

## Cross-Reference — Eastern Continent Holidays

| Kapuluan | Eastern Equivalent | Overlap |
|----------|-------------------|---------|
| Pista ng Araw (Midsummer, Day ~165) | Midsummer (Day 105, Solstice) | ~60 days apart. Both celebrate the sun. |
| Pista ng Ani (Harvest, Day ~195) | Harvest Eve / Ember Night (Day 195, Ashfall) | **Same day.** The Dawnwatch's Ember Night = Kapuluan's Harvest Festival. |
| Pista ng Pag-asa (Year's End, Day ~350) | Year's End (Day 360, Thawsend) | ~10 days apart. Both mark the turning of the year. |
| Pagpupuyat ng Bakunawa (Vigil, Day ~255) | Hearthwatch (Day 270, Frostmere) | ~15 days apart. Both are solemn watches in early winter. |

**Notable:** Harvest Eve and Pista ng Ani fall on the same day. The Dawnwatch's wedding was on Harvest Eve. If they had been in Kapuluan, it would have been the Harvest Festival. The calendars align.
